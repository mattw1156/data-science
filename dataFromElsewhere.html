We continue to look at the very rich by turning to a more systematic data source than Forbes magazine, the World Top Incomes Database hosted by the Paris School of Economics [http://topincomes.g-mond.parisschoolofeconomics.eu]. This is derived from income tax reports, and compiles information about the very highest incomes in various countries over time, trying as hard as possible to produce numbers that are comparable across time and space. For most countries in most time periods, the upper end of the income distribution roughly follows a Pareto distribution, with probability density function

\[f(x)=\frac{(a-1)x^{-a}}{x_{min}^{1-a}}\]

for incomes x ≥ x_{min}. (Typically, xmin is large enough that only the richest 3–4% of the population falls above it.) As the Pareto exponent a gets smaller, the distribution of income becomes more unequal, that is, more of the population’s total income is concentrated among the very richest people.

We will use this to estimate how income inequality changed in the US over the last hundred years or so. (Whether the trends are good or bad or a mix is beyond our scope here.) WTID exports its data sets as .xlsx spreadsheets. Problem 3. (10 points)

One can show from the earlier equations that one can estimate the exponent by the formula

\[ a = 1 - \frac{log 10}{log P99/P 99.9} \]

Write a function, exponent.est_ratio() which takes in values for P99 and P99.9, and returns the value of a implied by the above equation. Check that if P99=1e6 and P99.9=1e7, your function returns an a of 2.
Solution

Type your answer here

exponent.est = function(P99, P99.9){
  a = 1 - ((log(10)/(log(P99/P99.9))))
  return(a)
}
exponent.est(1e6, 1e7)

Problem 6. (10 points)

The logic leading the equation above also implies that

\[ (P99.5/P99.9)^{1-a}=5 \]

Write a function which takes P99.5, P99.9 and a, and calculates the left-hand side of that equation. Plot the values for each year, using the data and your estimates of the exponent. Add a horizontal line with vertical coordinate 5. How good is the fit?

Solution

Type your answer here

left = function(P99.5, P99.9, a){
  (P99.5/P99.9)^(1-a)
}
plot(data$Year, left(data$P99.5.income.threshold, data$P99.9.income.threshold, a), ylab = "lhs", xlab = "year")
abline(h = 5, col = "red",lwd = 3)
